Public-Key Cryptography Part 1

The Key Distribution Problem

Every symmetric cipher — DES, 3DES, AES — shares a fundamental assumption: both parties already have the same secret key. But how do two parties who have never communicated before establish that shared key? If they want to use AES to communicate securely, they first need the same 128-bit key — but sharing it over an insecure channel is precisely the problem they are trying to solve.

This is the key distribution problem, and it plagued cryptography for centuries. Public-key cryptography (also called asymmetric cryptography) solves this by using a mathematically related pair of keys: one public, one private. What one key encrypts, only the other can decrypt — and the two keys cannot practically be derived from each other.

Key Pairs

In public-key cryptography, every party has a key pair:

  • Public key ($PU$): Published openly. Anyone can have it. Think of it as a padlock anyone can snap shut.
  • Private key ($PR$): Kept secret. Only the owner knows it. Think of it as the key that opens the padlock.

The public key is used for locking (encryption or signature verification). The private key is used for unlocking (decryption or signing). Knowing the public key does not let you compute the private key — this is the computationally one-way property.

The Three Services of Public-Key Cryptography

Public-key cryptography provides three fundamental services that symmetric cryptography cannot easily replicate:

1. Confidentiality (A sending to B)

B publishes $PU_B$. A encrypts with B's public key. Only B — who holds $PR_B$ — can decrypt.

$$C = E(PU_B,\ M) \qquad M = D(PR_B,\ C)$$

2. Digital Signature / Authentication / Non-Repudiation

A signs with its own private key. Anyone with A's public key can verify. Only A could have created the signature — proving the message came from A (authentication) and that A cannot later deny it (non-repudiation).

$$C = E(PR_A,\ M) \qquad M = D(PU_A,\ C)$$

3. Confidentiality + Digital Signature

A signs first (with $PR_A$), then encrypts (with $PU_B$). B decrypts first, then verifies the signature.

$$C = E\!\left(PU_B,\ E(PR_A,\ M)\right) \qquad M = D\!\left(PU_A,\ D(PR_B,\ C)\right)$$

Does This Replace Symmetric Cryptography?

⚠️ Common Misconception

Public-key cryptography does not replace symmetric ciphers. RSA encrypting a large file is orders of magnitude slower than AES. In practice, public-key cryptography handles only two narrow tasks: authentication (signing) and key exchange (establishing a shared session key that AES then uses). The actual data is always encrypted with a fast symmetric cipher.

RSA — Mathematical Foundation Part 2

Euler's Theorem

RSA is built on a result from number theory. Euler's theorem states: for every positive integer $n$ and every integer $a$ that is coprime to $n$ (meaning $\gcd(a, n) = 1$):

$$a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod{n}$$

where $\phi(n)$ is Euler's totient function — the count of integers from 1 to $n$ that are coprime to $n$.

A useful consequence: any exponent that is a multiple of $\phi(n)$ contributes nothing — it cancels out. If $k = k_1 \cdot \phi(n) + k_2$:

$$a^k = a^{k_1 \cdot \phi(n) + k_2} = \left(a^{\phi(n)}\right)^{k_1} \cdot a^{k_2} \equiv 1^{k_1} \cdot a^{k_2} \equiv a^{k_2} \pmod{n}$$

The RSA Insight: Multiplicative Inverses Mod $\phi(n)$

Suppose we find two integers $e$ and $d$ that are multiplicative inverses modulo $\phi(n)$:

$$e \cdot d \equiv 1 \pmod{\phi(n)}$$

This means $e \cdot d = 1 + k \cdot \phi(n)$ for some integer $k$. Now apply Euler's theorem to a message $M$ (coprime to $n$):

$$M^{e \cdot d} = M^{1 + k \cdot \phi(n)} = M \cdot \left(M^{\phi(n)}\right)^k \equiv M \cdot 1^k \equiv M \pmod{n}$$

So encrypting with $e$ (raise to power $e$ mod $n$) and then decrypting with $d$ (raise to power $d$ mod $n$) recovers the original message exactly. This is the mathematical core of RSA.

Why $n = p \cdot q$ (Product of Two Primes)?

When $n$ is the product of two distinct primes $p$ and $q$, the totient simplifies beautifully:

$$n = p \times q \qquad \phi(n) = (p-1)(q-1)$$

Rivest, Shamir, and Adleman proved that the property $M^{e \cdot d} \equiv M \pmod{n}$ holds for all $M$ in $[0, n)$ when $n = pq$ — not just when $M$ is coprime to $n$. This guarantees RSA works for any plaintext.

🔐 The Security Principle

Multiplying $p \times q$ to get $n$ is easy. Factoring $n$ back into $p$ and $q$ is computationally infeasible for large primes. This asymmetry is the source of RSA's security. Anyone can compute $M^e \bmod n$ (encrypt), but only the person who knows $p$ and $q$ (and thus $\phi(n)$ and $d$) can decrypt.

🧠 Knowledge Check
What does Euler's theorem tell us about $a^{\phi(n)} \bmod n$?
Show Answer ▾
It equals 1 (as long as $\gcd(a, n) = 1$). Any multiple of $\phi(n)$ in the exponent cancels out to 1. This is why $M^{ed} \equiv M \pmod{n}$ when $ed \equiv 1 \pmod{\phi(n)}$.
If $p = 11$ and $q = 7$, what is $\phi(n)$?
Show Answer ▾
$\phi(n) = (p-1)(q-1) = 10 \times 6 = 60$. ($n = 77$.)

RSA Key Generation Part 3

The Complete Procedure

  • 1
    Choose two large distinct primes $p$ and $q$. These are generated using a random number generator and the Miller-Rabin primality test. They must be kept secret forever. If $n$ must be $B$ bits, each prime is roughly $B/2$ bits.
  • 2
    Compute the modulus: $$n = p \times q$$
  • 3
    Compute the totient: $$\phi(n) = (p-1)(q-1)$$
  • 4
    Choose the public exponent $e$ such that: $$1 < e < \phi(n) \quad \text{and} \quad \gcd(e, \phi(n)) = 1$$ Typical values: 65537 ($= 2^{16}+1$, strongly preferred), 17, 3 (insecure). 65537 is prime, binary $10000000000000001_2$ (only 2 set bits — very fast exponentiation).
  • 5
    Compute the private exponent $d$: $$d = e^{-1} \bmod \phi(n)$$ $d$ is the modular multiplicative inverse of $e$ modulo $\phi(n)$, computed using the Extended Euclidean Algorithm. It satisfies $e \cdot d \equiv 1 \pmod{\phi(n)}$.
  • 6
    Public Key = $[e,\ n]$ — publish this to the world.
  • 7
    Private Key = $[d,\ n]$ — guard this with your life.
⚠️ What Must Be Kept Secret

$d$, $p$, $q$, and $\phi(n)$ must ALL remain secret. Knowing $\phi(n)$ is essentially equivalent to knowing $p$ and $q$ — from $n$ and $\phi(n)$, one can solve for $p$ and $q$. Anyone who knows any of these can compute $d$ and break the cipher.

Why $e = 3$ Is Dangerous

If $e = 3$ and a message $M$ is short, the ciphertext $C = M^3 \bmod n$ may equal $M^3$ exactly (if $M^3 < n$, the modular reduction never triggers). An attacker simply takes the cube root of $C$ to recover $M$ — no factoring needed. This attack vanishes with $e = 65537$ and proper padding, since $M^{65537}$ always exceeds $n$ for any realistic message.

Choosing $e$ — The Coprimality Condition

$\gcd(e, \phi(n)) = 1$ is equivalent to requiring:

  • $\gcd(e, p-1) = 1$ — equivalently $p \bmod e \neq 1$
  • $\gcd(e, q-1) = 1$ — equivalently $q \bmod e \neq 1$

If you want a specific $e$ (like 65537), just generate $p$ and $q$ until both conditions hold — which happens quickly in practice.

RSA Encryption & Decryption Part 4

The Formulas

With the key pair established, RSA encryption and decryption are simply:

$$C = M^e \bmod n \quad \text{(Encrypt with public key } [e, n]\text{)}$$

$$M = C^d \bmod n \quad \text{(Decrypt with private key } [d, n]\text{)}$$

Both operations are modular exponentiation. The mathematical correctness follows from Euler's theorem: $C^d = (M^e)^d = M^{ed} \equiv M \pmod{n}$ because $ed \equiv 1 \pmod{\phi(n)}$.

Why Decryption Is Slower Than Encryption

The public exponent $e$ is chosen to be small with few set bits — for example, $e = 65537 = (10000000000000001)_2$ has only 2 bits set. Computing $M^e \bmod n$ requires at most about $2 \times 17 = 34$ multiplications using square-and-multiply.

The private exponent $d$, however, is computed as the modular inverse of $e$ and is roughly the same size as $n$ — often 2048 bits with approximately 1024 set bits. Computing $C^d \bmod n$ requires about $3 \times 2048 = 6144$ multiplications. This is why decryption is the computationally expensive operation in RSA, and why CRT is used to speed it up.

Block Size

When RSA is used as a block cipher, the block size is chosen to be smaller than $n$ — typically about half the number of bits needed to represent $n$. For example, a 1024-bit modulus allows roughly 512-bit message blocks (with padding filling the rest). The message integer $M$ must always satisfy $0 \leq M < n$.

📌 Message Padding is Essential

Before encrypting, the message is padded with random bytes to fill the entire block (using schemes like OAEP — Optimal Asymmetric Encryption Padding). This prevents attacks based on short messages, ensures the modular reduction always happens, and provides semantic security (same plaintext encrypts to different ciphertexts each time).

Fast Modular Exponentiation Part 5

Why Naive Computation Is Impossible

RSA requires computing $M^e \bmod n$. Even with a small $e = 65537$, the value $M^{65537}$ — before reduction — would have an astronomical number of digits. Computing it directly is impossible. The key insight: reduce modulo $n$ at every step, keeping numbers manageable throughout.

The Square-and-Multiply Algorithm

Express the exponent $B$ in binary: $B = (b_k b_{k-1} \ldots b_1 b_0)_2 = \sum_{b_i \neq 0} 2^i$

Then: $A^B = A^{\sum_{b_i \neq 0} 2^i} = \prod_{b_i \neq 0} A^{2^i}$

And since modular multiplication distributes:

$$A^B \bmod n = \prod_{b_i \neq 0} \left[A^{2^i} \bmod n\right] \bmod n$$ 
result = 1
base = A mod n
for each bit b of B (from LSB to MSB):
    if b == 1:
        result = (result × base) mod n
    base = (base × base) mod n
return result

This requires at most $2 \log_2 B$ multiplications — one squaring per bit, one extra multiplication per set bit. Numbers never grow larger than $n^2$.

RSA-CRT Decryption Speedup Part 6

The Problem

RSA decryption requires $M = C^d \bmod n$ where $d$ is roughly the same size as $n$ (often 2048+ bits). This is slow. The Chinese Remainder Theorem (CRT) provides approximately a 4× speedup by splitting the computation into two smaller modular exponentiations using $p$ and $q$ separately.

💡 Key Point: p and q Are Already Known

During decryption, the private key holder already knows $p$ and $q$ — they are the secret prime factors used to create the key. The CRT method uses these known factors to dramatically speed up computation. An attacker who doesn't know $p$ and $q$ cannot use CRT to break the cipher.

The CRT Decryption Formulas

Compute:

$$V_p = C^d \bmod p \qquad V_q = C^d \bmod q$$ $$X_p = q \cdot (q^{-1} \bmod p) \qquad X_q = p \cdot (p^{-1} \bmod q)$$ $$M = C^d \bmod n = (V_p \cdot X_p + V_q \cdot X_q) \bmod n$$

Further Speedup: Fermat's Little Theorem

For prime $p$, Fermat's Little Theorem states $a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$. So when computing $C^d \bmod p$, write $d = u(p-1) + v$ and reduce: $C^d \equiv C^v \pmod{p}$. This makes the exponents even smaller.

Combined CRT + FLT reduces the cost to roughly one-quarter of naive $C^d \bmod n$.

What Is NOT Part of CRT Decryption

❌ Common Exam Trap

"Factor $n$ dynamically during decryption" is NOT part of RSA-CRT. The legitimate key holder already knows $p$ and $q$ — they were used to generate the key. Factoring $n$ is what an attacker would need to do. The whole point of RSA security is that factoring $n$ is infeasible for outsiders.

🧠 Knowledge Check
Why does CRT provide a ~4x speedup for RSA decryption?
Show Answer ▾
CRT replaces one large exponentiation ($C^d \bmod n$ with 2048-bit numbers) with two smaller ones ($C^d \bmod p$ and $C^d \bmod q$, each with ~1024-bit numbers). Since the cost of modular multiplication scales with the square of the number size, halving the size cuts each multiplication to 1/4 the cost. Two such multiplications instead of one large one gives approximately a 4x speedup overall.

RSA Security — Threats & Vulnerabilities Part 7

1. The Mathematical Foundation — Factoring

RSA's security depends entirely on the practical infeasibility of factoring the modulus $n = pq$. Various algorithms exist (trial division, Fermat's method, quadratic sieve, number field sieve, Pollard's rho), but none can practically factor numbers of modern RSA sizes.

RSA-640640Factored 2005

RSA-20482048$200K prize — unclaimed

Current recommendation: 2048-bit minimum; 3072–4096 bits for long-term security.

2. Chosen Ciphertext / Small Exponent Attack

If $e = 3$ and a message $M$ is short enough that $M^3 < n$, then $C = M^3$ (no modular reduction). An attacker simply computes $\sqrt[3]{C}$ to recover $M$ — no factoring required.

Defense: Message padding (OAEP) ensures the padded message is always large, so $M^3 \bmod n$ always reduces. Also, prefer $e = 65537$ over $e = 3$.

3. Low Entropy — Shared Prime Factor Attack

If two different RSA moduli $n_1$ and $n_2$ share a common prime factor $p$, then $\gcd(n_1, n_2) = p$ — computable in milliseconds. Both private keys are immediately broken.

⚠️ Real-World Impact

A 2012 study ("Mining your Ps and Qs") harvested 5+ million TLS certificates and 4 million SSH keys. Using only GCD computations, they recovered the private keys for 0.50% of all TLS servers and 0.03% of SSH servers. Root cause: devices generating keys at boot time had low entropy (little randomness), causing some pairs of devices to generate primes with a shared factor.

WORKED EXAMPLE Shared Factor Attack

Given: $n_1 = 221$ and $n_2 = 247$

Compute $\gcd(221, 247)$:

  • $247 = 1 \times 221 + 26$
  • $221 = 8 \times 26 + 13$
  • $26 = 2 \times 13 + 0$ → $\gcd = 13$

Shared factor: $p = 13$. Factor both: $n_1 = 13 \times 17$, $n_2 = 13 \times 19$.

Both private keys are broken with a single GCD computation.

4. Perfect Forward Secrecy — RSA's Limitation

When RSA is used for key exchange (A encrypts session key $K_S$ with B's public key), a recording eavesdropper who later obtains B's private key can decrypt all recorded session keys and therefore all past conversations. RSA-based key exchange has no forward secrecy.

The Diffie-Hellman key exchange (covered in Lecture 04) solves this with ephemeral keys that are discarded after each session.

🧠 Knowledge Check
Why does increasing the key size provide stronger RSA security?
Show Answer ▾
Larger $n = pq$ means larger primes $p$ and $q$, making factoring computationally harder. The best known factoring algorithms have sub-exponential but super-polynomial complexity — each additional bit of key size disproportionately increases the difficulty of factoring.
Explain the low entropy vulnerability in one sentence.
Show Answer ▾
If two devices generate RSA keys using near-identical random seeds (due to low entropy at boot time), their key moduli may share a common prime factor, which an attacker can find instantly using the GCD algorithm — breaking both private keys.

EXAM Q31Key Generation Order
What is the correct order for RSA key generation?
aSelect $e$ → Choose $n$ → Factor $n$ → Compute $d$
bGenerate $d$ → Find $e$ → Randomize $n$ → Pick $p, q$
cCompute $n$ → Solve for $p, q$ → Derive $e$ → Choose $d$
dChoose primes $p, q$ → Compute $n = pq$ → Select $e$ → Calculate $d$
Answer: (d). The mandatory order: choose $p$ and $q$ first → compute $n = pq$ and $\phi(n) = (p-1)(q-1)$ → select $e$ coprime to $\phi(n)$ → compute $d = e^{-1} \bmod \phi(n)$. Each step depends on the previous one. You cannot choose $e$ before knowing $\phi(n)$, and you cannot compute $d$ before choosing $e$.
EXAM Q32Condition on $e$
In RSA, what condition must the public exponent $e$ satisfy?
a$e > n$
b$e$ must be prime
c$1 < e < \phi(n)$ and $\gcd(e, \phi(n)) = 1$

Answer: (c). $e$ must be in the range $(1, \phi(n))$ and coprime to $\phi(n)$. The coprimality condition guarantees $d = e^{-1} \bmod \phi(n)$ exists (a modular inverse exists if and only if $\gcd(e, \phi(n)) = 1$). $e$ need not be prime — though common choices (3, 17, 65537) happen to be prime, this is for efficiency reasons, not a mathematical requirement.
EXAM Q33Computing $d$
How is the private exponent $d$ computed in RSA?
a$d \equiv e^{-1} \bmod \phi(n)$
b$d = n$
c$d = e \bmod p$
d$d$ is randomly selected
Answer: (a). $d$ is the modular multiplicative inverse of $e$ modulo $\phi(n)$, satisfying $e \cdot d \equiv 1 \pmod{\phi(n)}$. It is computed deterministically using the Extended Euclidean Algorithm — not randomly selected. $d$ cannot be derived without knowing $\phi(n)$, which requires knowing $p$ and $q$.
EXAM Q34Expensive Operation
Which RSA operation is computationally expensive?
aDecryption (using $d$)
bEncryption (using $e$)
cGenerating $p$ and $q$
dCalculating $\gcd$
Answer: (a). Decryption requires $C^d \bmod n$. The private exponent $d$ is as large as $n$ (typically 2048+ bits), requiring thousands of modular multiplications. Encryption with a small $e$ (like 65537 with only 2 set bits) requires only about 34 multiplications. CRT can speed up decryption by ~4x but it is still much slower than encryption.
EXAM Q35Why RSA Not for Bulk Encryption
Why is RSA rarely used for bulk (message content) encryption?
aLack of authentication
bSlow performance
cFixed block size
dInability to encrypt large files
Answer: (b). RSA's modular exponentiation is orders of magnitude slower than symmetric encryption (AES). In practice, RSA is used only for authentication and for encrypting a small session key. The actual data is encrypted with AES using that session key. Note: (d) is technically true but it's a consequence of the performance problem, not the root reason.
EXAM Q36Common Modulus Attack
Which vulnerability arises from reusing the same modulus $n$ with different public exponents $e$?
aNonce reuse
bTiming attack
cPadding oracle attack
dCommon modulus attack
Answer: (d). If two parties use the same $n$ with different exponents $e_1$, $e_2$ where $\gcd(e_1, e_2) = 1$, and the same message $M$ is encrypted to both: find $a, b$ such that $e_1 a + e_2 b = 1$ (Extended Euclid). Then $C_1^a \cdot C_2^b = (M^{e_1})^a (M^{e_2})^b = M^{e_1 a + e_2 b} = M^1 = M \pmod{n}$. The message is recovered without factoring $n$.
EXAM Q37CRT Purpose
What is the primary purpose of using the Chinese Remainder Theorem (CRT) with RSA?
aTo increase the key size
bTo enable quantum resistance
cTo replace modular exponentiation
dTo speed up decryption/signature generation by approximately 4x
Answer: (d). CRT splits $C^d \bmod n$ into two smaller exponentiations: $C^d \bmod p$ and $C^d \bmod q$. Since $p$ and $q$ are half the size of $n$, each multiplication is 4x cheaper (quadratic scaling). Combined with Fermat's Little Theorem (which further reduces the exponents), the overall speedup is approximately 4x. Modular exponentiation is still used — CRT makes it faster, not obsolete.

Answer: (b). The private key holder already knows $p$ and $q$ — they were used when the key was generated and are stored as part of the private key. "Factor $n$ dynamically" is exactly what an attacker would need to do to break RSA. The legitimate decryptor never factors $n$ during normal operation; they simply use the pre-known $p$ and $q$.
