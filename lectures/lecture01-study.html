Core Vocabulary — The Language of Cryptography

Why Vocabulary Matters

Before you can understand any cryptographic algorithm, you need to be precise about the words you use. Cryptography has a well-defined vocabulary, and mixing up even two terms — say, "key" and "algorithm," or "encryption" and "ciphertext" — causes genuine confusion in both exams and real engineering. So let's build the vocabulary carefully, starting from first principles.

What Problem Are We Solving?

Imagine Alice wants to send a private message to Bob over the internet. The internet is a public network — anyone monitoring the connection can potentially read every packet of data that flows through it. Alice and Bob need a way to transform the message so that an eavesdropper who intercepts it cannot make sense of it, while Bob can still recover the original. This transformation is encryption.

The original message — whatever Alice wants to say — is called the plaintext. It doesn't have to be English text; it could be a video, a bank transaction record, a password. "Plain" simply means unprotected and readable. The result of encrypting the plaintext is called the ciphertext. An important detail: in most modern ciphers, the ciphertext is the same size as the plaintext. You might expect encryption to compress or expand data dramatically, but block ciphers preserve the block size. The difference is that the ciphertext looks like random nonsense to anyone without the key.

The Algorithm vs. the Key — A Critical Distinction

The encryption algorithm is the specific sequence of computational steps that transforms plaintext into ciphertext. Think of it as a recipe — a procedure that anyone can, in principle, follow. The secret key is the parameter (or set of parameters) that controls how the algorithm behaves for a specific communication. Given the same plaintext, two different keys produce two completely different ciphertexts. Without knowing the key, even knowing the complete algorithm does not help you recover the plaintext.

This is a crucial point: the algorithm should be public; the key should be secret. This is called Kerckhoffs's Principle, and it is the cornerstone of all modern cryptography. We will explore it more deeply in the next section.

Block Ciphers vs. Stream Ciphers

A block cipher divides plaintext into fixed-size chunks called blocks and processes one complete block at a time. AES processes 128-bit (16-byte) blocks — every 16 bytes of plaintext in, every 16 bytes of ciphertext out. The entire block is transformed as a unit; every bit of the output depends on every bit of the input within that block. This is what produces the strong mixing properties we need for security.

A stream cipher encrypts data continuously — typically one byte, or even one bit, at a time — as it flows through the cipher. It generates a long sequence of pseudo-random bits called a keystream (derived from the key) and XORs this keystream with the plaintext to produce ciphertext. RC4, which you will encounter in Assignment 2, is a stream cipher. Stream ciphers are naturally suited to real-time communications where data arrives byte-by-byte and you cannot wait for a full block. Block ciphers are better for files and stored data — though block ciphers can be placed in special modes (like CTR mode) that convert them into stream-cipher-like behavior.

Cryptanalysis, Cryptography, and Cryptology

Cryptography is the broad field of designing encryption and decryption schemes. Cryptanalysis is the art of breaking cryptographic systems — recovering the plaintext or the key without authorized access. You might wonder: is cryptanalysis only for attackers? Absolutely not. Security researchers perform cryptanalysis on new ciphers to find weaknesses before those ciphers are deployed at scale. The process of designing a cipher, attacking it, fixing it, and attacking again is how the field makes progress. Cryptology is the parent discipline encompassing both.

The Key Space

The key space is the total number of distinct keys a cipher can use. For an n-bit key, the key space has 2ⁿ members. The key space size determines how resistant a cipher is to brute-force attack — trying every key until one works. AES with a 128-bit key has a key space of 2¹²⁸ ≈ 3.4 × 10³⁸. At one trillion keys per second (10¹²), trying all of them would take 10²⁶ seconds — vastly longer than the age of the universe.

But here is the crucial insight: a large key space is necessary but not sufficient for security. The monoalphabetic cipher has a key space of 26! ≈ 4 × 10²⁶ — dramatically larger than DES — yet it can be broken in minutes. Key space protects against brute-force; it does not protect against statistical or algebraic attacks. This theme will repeat throughout the entire lecture.

Symmetric vs. Asymmetric Cryptography — and Why the Algorithm Must Be Public

The Counterintuitive Principle

One of the first genuine "aha" moments in learning cryptography is this: the encryption algorithm should be published openly — available to everyone, including your adversaries. If you have never studied security before, this sounds absurd. Surely the more secret your system, the more secure it is?

Think through why the opposite is true. If the algorithm is secret, who can test whether it is actually secure? Only you. But security is defined by an adversary's inability to break it — and adversaries are creative, persistent, and have different skills than you. History is full of "secret" cryptographic systems that had catastrophic weaknesses their designers never imagined. The GSM mobile encryption algorithm was reverse-engineered and found to be weak. Various proprietary encryption schemes for video games, ATMs, and military hardware have all failed because they were never publicly scrutinized.

By contrast, when you publish your algorithm, every cryptographer in the world can attack it. Those attacks either expose weaknesses (which you fix) or fail (which builds confidence). AES was selected through an open international competition where teams worldwide submitted algorithms and attacked each other's for years. The winner survived every attack. That public process is exactly what makes us trust AES today. This principle — that security must rest on the secrecy of the key, not the algorithm — is called Kerckhoffs's Principle, named after 19th-century cryptographer Auguste Kerckhoffs.

Symmetric Key Cryptography

In symmetric (classical) cryptography, Alice and Bob share the same secret key. Alice uses it to encrypt; Bob uses the same key to decrypt. The algorithms are typically fast — AES can encrypt at multiple gigabytes per second on modern hardware. The challenge is purely logistical: how do Alice and Bob agree on a shared key in the first place? If they have no pre-existing secure channel, they face the key distribution problem. This was the defining unsolved problem of cryptography for thousands of years. Armies needed couriers to physically deliver codebooks. Banks needed secure telephone lines to set up keys.

Asymmetric (Public Key) Cryptography

In asymmetric (public key) cryptography, invented in the 1970s, each party has two mathematically linked keys: a public key (published openly — anyone can have it) and a private key (kept absolutely secret). Data encrypted with the public key can only be decrypted with the corresponding private key. Given only the public key, it is computationally infeasible to derive the private key.

This solves the key distribution problem beautifully: Bob publishes his public key. Alice encrypts with Bob's public key. Only Bob can decrypt, because only Bob has the private key. They never needed a pre-existing secure channel.

The trade-off is performance. RSA (a widely used asymmetric cipher) is roughly 1000× slower than AES. For this reason, real-world systems use asymmetric cryptography only to securely exchange a symmetric key, then switch to that symmetric key for bulk data. This hybrid approach is exactly what TLS (the protocol securing all HTTPS websites) does.

Cryptanalysis — The Art and Science of Breaking Ciphers

Why This Belongs in a Security Course

You might wonder why a course on security spends time teaching you how to break things. The answer is that every design decision in a modern cipher — every step of AES, every parameter of RSA — was made in direct response to a known attack. If you don't understand the attacks, the design choices look arbitrary. Once you understand the attacks, the design becomes obviously motivated.

Think of it like building a lock: you cannot design a good lock unless you understand how people pick locks. The history of cryptography is an arms race between cipher designers and cryptanalysts, with each side driving the other to greater sophistication. The lecture slides cover four main attack types: brute-force, codebook, algebraic, and statistical. Each one exploits a different weakness.

Brute-Force Attack

The simplest possible attack: systematically try every key in the key space until you find one that produces intelligible plaintext when used to decrypt the ciphertext. This requires no cleverness — just computation. The feasibility depends entirely on key space size.

With a 56-bit DES key: there are 2⁵⁶ ≈ 7.2 × 10¹⁶ possible keys. At 10⁹ keys per second: average tries = 2⁵⁵ ≈ 3.6 × 10¹⁶ keys → time ≈ 3.6 × 10⁷ seconds ≈ 13 months. With a one-million-core parallel machine: roughly 37 seconds. In 1998, the EFF built a dedicated machine for $250,000 that cracked DES in under 56 hours. Today it could be done for far less.

With AES's 128-bit key: at 10¹⁸ keys/second (the combined speed of all computers on Earth, generously estimated), it would take ~10²⁰ seconds ≈ 10¹³ years — thousands of times the age of the universe. Brute-force is not a viable attack against AES.

Key insight for exams: brute-force has minimal memory requirements but potentially enormous time requirements.

Codebook Attack

A completely different approach: instead of trying keys, the attacker tries to collect as many known plaintext–ciphertext pairs as possible and build a lookup table (the "codebook") that maps plaintexts directly to ciphertexts. Once the codebook is large enough, future ciphertext can be decrypted instantly by table lookup — without ever knowing or finding the key.

You might wonder: how does an attacker get plaintext–ciphertext pairs? More often than you'd think. A standard greeting ("Dear all,"), a fixed file header, a timestamp in a known format — these are known plaintexts that appear in encrypted messages. Wartime cryptanalysis was full of such "cribs" (known plaintext fragments).

The limitation: memory. A complete codebook for a 64-bit block cipher would require 2⁶⁴ entries of 64 bits each — roughly 10²¹ bits of storage. For 128-bit blocks, it's astronomically more absurd. Codebook attack in contrast to brute-force: enormous memory, but instantaneous results once built.

Algebraic Attack

The attacker expresses the mathematical relationship between plaintext, ciphertext, and key as a system of equations, then solves for the key. This works when the cipher has exploitable algebraic structure — particularly if some operation is linear. The Hill cipher, which you will study shortly, is a textbook example: it is entirely based on linear matrix multiplication, so a few known plaintext–ciphertext pairs allow the attacker to solve directly for the key matrix. No search required at all.

This is why AES is designed with deliberate, strong non-linearity. The SubBytes step (the AES S-box) is constructed using finite field mathematics to ensure there is no simple algebraic relationship between inputs and outputs. Every design choice in AES has a counterpart attack it prevents.

The Time–Memory Tradeoff

Real attackers rarely operate at either extreme of the brute-force / codebook spectrum. They precompute partial codebooks — computing and storing as many pairs as their memory budget allows, then using those stored values to speed up the search. Rainbow tables (used to attack password hashes) are the famous real-world example: instead of storing all possible passwords and their hashes (too much memory), or computing the hash for every guess during the attack (too much time), rainbow tables precompute chains that compress storage while retaining most of the speed benefit.

The time–memory tradeoff matters because an attacker has finite resources. They choose how to balance computation time against storage space. System designers must consider both dimensions — not just "how long does brute-force take?" but also "how much precomputation + storage would enable a practical attack?"

The Caesar Cipher

Building Intuition from Scratch

Julius Caesar, according to Suetonius, protected military communications using a simple substitution: replace each letter with the letter three positions further along in the alphabet. A becomes D, B becomes E, ..., X becomes A, Y becomes B, Z becomes C. When you reach the end, you wrap around.

Before writing any formula, make sure the physical intuition is clear. Imagine writing the alphabet in a circle. To encrypt a letter, move the pointer clockwise by 3 steps and write down where you land. To decrypt, move counter-clockwise by 3 steps. The number 3 is the key.

The generalization: instead of always shifting by 3, let the shift amount k be any number from 0 to 25. This gives the general Caesar cipher (also called a shift cipher or ROT-cipher). The key k is the secret — but with only 26 possible values (0 through 25), the key space is tiny.

The Mathematics

Assign each letter an integer: A=0, B=1, ..., Z=25. Encryption and decryption become simple modular arithmetic operations:

c = E(k, p) = (p + k) mod 26     p = D(k, c) = (c − k) mod 26

The mod 26 handles the circular wraparound. When you go past Z (position 25), you return to A (position 0). For example: encrypt 'Y' (position 24) with k=3: (24+3) mod 26 = 27 mod 26 = 1 = 'B'. Correct — Y wraps around to B.

For decryption, don't worry about negative numbers in modular arithmetic: (c − k) mod 26 will always give the correct result. In Python, the % operator handles negative values correctly. In other languages you may need: ((c − k) % 26 + 26) % 26.

Why the Caesar Cipher Fails Completely

The Caesar cipher has only 26 possible keys. A human working by hand can try all 26 decryptions in a few minutes — you don't need any sophisticated analysis. Just try k=0, 1, 2, ..., 25 and look for the one that produces readable English. The key space is so small that the concept of "security" barely applies.

The important lesson isn't "Caesar is bad" — it's "insufficient key space immediately destroys a cipher." Key space is the first thing you check, but as we'll see with the monoalphabetic cipher, it isn't the last.

The Monoalphabetic Cipher & Statistical Attacks

Making the Key Space Enormous

The Caesar cipher fails because of its tiny key space: only 26 shifts. A natural response is to make the substitution completely arbitrary. Instead of "always shift by k," define any mapping of the 26 letters: A→Q, B→Z, C→M, and so on — any permutation. The only rule is that the mapping must be one-to-one (each plaintext letter maps to a unique ciphertext letter), so that decryption is unambiguous. This is the monoalphabetic substitution cipher.

The key is now the complete substitution table — a specific permutation of 26 letters. The number of distinct permutations is 26! ≈ 4 × 10²⁶. This is immensely larger than DES's 2⁵⁶ ≈ 7.2 × 10¹⁶. Even testing one billion permutations per second, trying half the key space would take longer than the age of the universe. Brute-force is completely hopeless.

And yet this cipher can be broken by a human, by hand, with pencil and paper, in a matter of minutes.

The Fatal Flaw: Frequency Preservation

Here is the core insight: the cipher changes which symbol represents a letter, but it preserves how often that letter appears.

In any sufficiently long piece of English text, the letter 'E' appears approximately 12.7% of the time. 'T' appears about 9.1%. 'A' about 8.2%. The frequencies follow a characteristic distribution for English (and a different but equally characteristic distribution for French, German, Spanish, etc.).

Now suppose 'E' is mapped to 'K' in the substitution table. Every 'E' in the plaintext becomes a 'K' in the ciphertext. Since 'E' appears 12.7% of the time, 'K' now also appears 12.7% of the time. The frequency is preserved — just attached to a different symbol. An attacker who intercepts the ciphertext can count letter frequencies, match the most frequent ciphertext letter to 'E', the second most frequent to 'T', and so on. After guessing a few high-frequency letters, common words start to emerge, and the substitution table fills in rapidly.

This technique — frequency analysis — was described by Arab scholar Al-Kindi in the 9th century, over a thousand years ago. It works on any monoalphabetic cipher, regardless of how complex the substitution is.

Digrams and Trigrams

Single-letter frequency is just the beginning. Pairs of adjacent letters called digrams appear with characteristic frequencies in English: TH, HE, IN, ER, AN, RE, ON, EN are the most common. Triples called trigrams (THE, AND, ING, ION, ENT) are even more distinctive. A monoalphabetic cipher preserves these digram and trigram frequencies too. Looking for the most common ciphertext trigram and guessing it is "THE" immediately gives you three letter identifications at once.

The combined analysis of single letters, digrams, and trigrams, plus pattern matching for common short words ("A", "I", "THE", "AND"), can break a monoalphabetic cipher from as few as 25–30 characters of ciphertext. The key insight for the exam: a large key space does not guarantee security. The cipher must also destroy the statistical structure of the plaintext.

🎯 The Critical Lesson
The monoalphabetic cipher has a key space of 26! ≈ 4 × 10²⁶ — much larger than DES — yet it is broken in minutes. Key space size determines only resistance to brute-force. It says nothing about resistance to statistical analysis. True security requires both a large key space AND destruction of statistical / algebraic structure in the plaintext.
❓ True or False: The monoalphabetic cipher is secure because it has 26! ≈ 4 × 10²⁶ keys — too many to brute-force.▾
False. While brute-force is indeed impractical, "secure against brute-force" and "secure" are completely different claims. The cipher preserves the frequency distribution of letters, digrams, and trigrams, allowing an attacker to use frequency analysis — a technique that doesn't try any keys at all. Brute-force resistance is necessary but not sufficient.

The Playfair Cipher

The Motivation: Attack the Digram, Not the Letter

The monoalphabetic cipher fails because individual letter frequencies are preserved. A natural response: encrypt not individual letters but pairs of letters. If you encrypt the pair "TH" as a unit, then the frequency of "T" alone and the frequency of "H" alone no longer directly appear in the ciphertext. The attacker must now analyse digram frequencies — there are 26² = 676 possible digrams rather than just 26 letters, so far more ciphertext is needed before the frequencies are reliable.

This is the core idea of the Playfair cipher, invented by Charles Wheatstone in 1854. The British Army used it in WWI; Allied forces used it in WWII. It encrypts plaintext two letters at a time using a 5×5 matrix as the key.

Building the Key Matrix

Choose an encryption key — a word or phrase with no repeated letters. Construct a 5×5 matrix (25 cells for 25 distinct letters — I and J share one cell since the alphabet has 26 letters but we need exactly 25):

  1. Write the key's letters left-to-right, top-to-bottom, skipping any duplicates.
  2. Fill remaining cells with unused alphabet letters in alphabetical order.
  3. I and J share one cell — during encryption, J is always treated as I.

Split plaintext into consecutive pairs. First:

Repeated pair fix: If a pair would be the same letter twice (e.g., "LL"), insert filler 'X' between them: L·X·L·...

Three Encryption Rules
Rule 1 — Same Row: Both letters in the same row → replace each with the letter immediately to its right, wrapping around from col 4 back to col 0.

Rule 2 — Same Column: Both letters in the same column → replace each with the letter immediately below, wrapping from row 4 back to row 0.

Rule 3 — Rectangle: Different row AND different column → each letter takes the letter in its own row but in the other letter's column. (Swap columns, keep rows.)

The Hill Cipher

The Algebraic Leap

The Hill cipher, introduced by Lester Hill in 1929, takes a fundamentally different approach from Caesar and Playfair. Rather than applying clever but ad-hoc rules to letter pairs, Hill applies formal linear algebra — matrix multiplication over modular arithmetic. This lets you encrypt not just pairs but arbitrarily large groups of letters simultaneously, each output letter depending on every input letter in the group.

The motivation: the more letters you mix together at once, the more each output letter depends on many input letters simultaneously, and the harder it becomes to extract frequency information from the ciphertext. With a 3×3 key matrix, three letters are mixed at a time; with a 10×10 matrix, ten letters at a time.

How It Works

Assign integers 0–25 to letters A–Z as before. Choose a key matrix K (e.g., 3×3). To encrypt three plaintext letters [p₁, p₂, p₃], compute: [c₁, c₂, c₃]ᵀ = K × [p₁, p₂, p₃]ᵀ (mod 26). For decryption: [p₁, p₂, p₃]ᵀ = K⁻¹ × [c₁, c₂, c₃]ᵀ (mod 26). For decryption to work, det(K) must be coprime to 26 so that K⁻¹ (mod 26) exists.

Extreme Strength Against Ciphertext-Only Attack; Catastrophic Weakness Against Known-Plaintext

With a 3×3 matrix, there are roughly 26⁹ ≈ 5.4 × 10¹² possible keys — far too many to brute-force. Single-letter frequency analysis is useless because each output letter depends on three input letters at once.

However, the Hill cipher is completely linear. This means that given even a small number of known plaintext–ciphertext pairs, an attacker can set up the matrix equation K × P = C (mod 26) and solve for K directly using standard linear algebra. With three known plaintext triplets (giving a 3×3 plaintext matrix P), if P is invertible mod 26, the key is recovered immediately: K = C × P⁻¹ (mod 26). No search, no guesswork — just solving equations.

This teaches one of the most important lessons in cryptography: linear systems are fundamentally insecure. This is precisely why AES's SubBytes step was designed to be strongly non-linear — it specifically prevents the algebraic attack that trivially destroys the Hill cipher.

❓ Why is the Hill cipher secure against ciphertext-only attacks but insecure against known-plaintext attacks?▾
Against ciphertext-only: the attacker has no plaintext reference, the large key space (26⁹) prevents brute-force, and the multi-letter mixing disrupts single-letter frequency analysis. Against known-plaintext: the cipher is entirely linear. Given K × P = C, solving for K is trivial linear algebra — just compute K = C × P⁻¹ (mod 26). Linearity makes the key completely exposed once any plaintext–ciphertext pairs are known.

The Vigenère Cipher — The First Polyalphabetic Solution

The Key Concept: Monoalphabetic vs. Polyalphabetic

Every cipher studied so far — Caesar, monoalphabetic, Playfair, Hill — has one thing in common: a fixed, consistent relationship between plaintext and ciphertext symbols. If 'E' maps to 'K' in one position, it maps to 'K' in every position. The substitution rule is the same throughout the entire message. This is what "monoalphabetic" means: one substitution alphabet, used everywhere.

The fundamental consequence: frequency analysis always works on monoalphabetic ciphers. No matter how complex the substitution — individual letters, letter pairs, letter triplets — if the same rule is applied everywhere, the frequencies of symbols are preserved from plaintext to ciphertext.

The Vigenère cipher breaks this constraint by using a polyalphabetic substitution: the substitution rule changes at every character position, controlled by a repeating keyword. Two identical plaintext letters in different positions will (usually) produce different ciphertext letters.

How It Works

Choose a keyword — for example, abracadabra. Align it with the plaintext, repeating it as needed. Each letter of the keyword defines a Caesar cipher shift for the corresponding position: 'a' = shift 0, 'b' = shift 1, 'c' = shift 2, 'r' = shift 17, etc.

cᵢ = (pᵢ + kᵢ) mod 26     pᵢ = (cᵢ − kᵢ) mod 26

The Vigenère cipher is not one substitution alphabet but as many substitution alphabets as the key length. With a key of length N, position 1 uses one Caesar shift, position 2 a different shift, ..., position N yet another, then it repeats. The same plaintext letter in different positions gets encrypted differently depending on which key letter it aligns with.

Why This Defeats Frequency Analysis

Consider what happens to 'E' (the most common letter, appearing ~12.7% of the time in English). In a monoalphabetic cipher, every 'E' maps to the same ciphertext letter. In Vigenère with key abracadabra (length 11), 'E' is encrypted differently depending on which key position it falls on: aligned with 'a' → 'E', with 'b' → 'F', with 'r' → 'V', with 'c' → 'G', etc.

Now the 12.7% frequency of 'E' is spread across multiple ciphertext letters. No single ciphertext letter accumulates the full frequency of 'E'. The longer the key, the better this masking effect, and the harder frequency analysis becomes. An attacker must first determine the key length N (using the Kasiski test or index of coincidence), then split the ciphertext into N groups and attack each group separately as a monoalphabetic cipher. This is much harder than attacking a monoalphabetic cipher directly.

The Ultimate Form: The One-Time Pad

What if the key were as long as the message and never reused? With a key of length equal to the plaintext, there is no repetition — the cipher never cycles back to any previously used substitution. If the key letters are chosen uniformly at random (and never reused), the resulting ciphertext is statistically perfectly random. No amount of ciphertext analysis can reveal anything about the plaintext, because for any possible plaintext P, there exists exactly one key K that would encrypt P into the observed ciphertext C.

This is the one-time pad — the only cipher proven to be information-theoretically secure (secure against an attacker with unlimited computational power). A Vigenère cipher with a random key as long as the message IS a one-time pad. The exam question: "What happens if the keyword in the Vigenère cipher is as long as the plaintext and never reused?" — Answer: it becomes unbreakable, equivalent to a one-time pad.

The practical problem: the key must be as long as the message, truly random, and never reused. Sharing such a key requires a secure channel — which defeats the purpose if you already have one. These limitations restrict the one-time pad to very specific high-security scenarios (nuclear command and control, diplomatic hotlines, etc.).

Transposition Techniques

The Other Primitive Operation

Every cipher we've studied so far is a substitution cipher — it changes what letters appear. Transposition (also called permutation) takes the opposite approach: it changes where letters appear, while leaving the letters themselves unchanged. In a transposition cipher, the same set of plaintext letters is present in the ciphertext — just rearranged in a different order according to the key.

The simplest form is the column transposition cipher: write the plaintext across the rows of a matrix of fixed width; the key defines the order in which to read back the columns. For example, with a 5-column matrix and key order 4-1-3-2-5, you write the plaintext in rows and then read the columns in the order: column 4 first, then column 1, then column 3, etc.

Lecture convention note: in this course, key digits are used as the direct column read order. So key 3 1 4 2 means read column 3, then 1, then 4, then 2.

Why Transposition Alone Is Insufficient

Transposition preserves all individual letter frequencies perfectly. If 'E' appears 12.7% of the time in the plaintext, it still appears 12.7% of the time in the ciphertext — just in different positions. A frequency analysis of individual letters immediately reveals the underlying language and provides strong constraints on the cipher's structure.

But this doesn't mean transposition is useless. Combined with substitution, transposition becomes powerful. Substitution destroys frequency information; transposition then scrambles position information. When you apply both in multiple rounds, each operation amplifies the obscuring effect of the other. This is precisely what modern block ciphers do: AES uses substitution (SubBytes) and permutation (ShiftRows, MixColumns) in ten consecutive rounds. This is Claude Shannon's two properties: confusion (from substitution) and diffusion (from permutation), which you will study in the DES section.

❓ What is the difference between substitution and transposition ciphers?▾
A substitution cipher replaces each plaintext element with a different element (the what changes, the where stays). A transposition/permutation cipher rearranges the positions of the elements (the where changes, the what stays). Substitution alone preserves positions; transposition alone preserves frequencies. Modern ciphers (AES) use both in multiple rounds to achieve genuine security.

The Ideal Block Cipher

Defining "Perfect" Security

Before designing a real block cipher, it helps to think about what the ideal block cipher would look like — even if it's not practically realizable. This defines the security target that real ciphers like AES try to approximate.

Consider a block cipher that processes N-bit blocks. The ideal block cipher would have this property: for each possible key, the relationship between input blocks and output blocks is a completely random, independent permutation of all possible N-bit strings. No patterns, no correlations, no algebraic relationships — pure randomness, subject only to the constraint that the mapping is invertible (so decryption works). An attacker facing such a cipher could not use frequency analysis (no statistical structure), algebraic attacks (no mathematical relationship), or codebook attacks without a complete codebook. The only option would be brute-force over the key space.

Why the Ideal Cipher Is Impractical

In an ideal block cipher, the encryption key is the codebook — the complete specification of which input maps to which output. For a 64-bit block cipher, there are 2⁶⁴ possible input blocks. The key must specify a 64-bit output for each one. The codebook size: 64 bits × 2⁶⁴ entries ≈ 10²¹ bits ≈ 100 billion terabytes. You cannot store this key, transmit it, or compute it quickly. The ideal block cipher is conceptually perfect but practically impossible.

This creates the fundamental tension in block cipher design: we want the behavior to look like an ideal cipher (completely random-looking output), but we need to achieve this with a tiny key (128 bits for AES) and fast computation. AES solves this with a carefully designed algorithm that produces output indistinguishable from random to any computationally bounded observer, using only a 128-bit key and a deterministic algorithm that runs in microseconds.

❓ Why is the ideal block cipher impractical, and what does this tell us about real block cipher design?▾
The ideal cipher's key IS the codebook — a complete table of all 2ⁿ input→output mappings for an n-bit block. For n=64, this requires ~10²¹ bits of storage. Real ciphers must use a compact key (e.g. 128 bits) and a fast algorithm that simulates the randomness of an ideal cipher — passing as a pseudorandom permutation to any efficient attacker. This is what "cryptographic security" means for block ciphers: behavioral indistinguishability from an ideal cipher.

The Feistel Structure

The Engineering Problem

Imagine you are a cryptographer at IBM in 1971, tasked with designing a cipher for banking. Your wish-list is clear: strong non-linear substitution operations for security, fast execution, and — critically for hardware cost — the same circuitry handling both encryption and decryption.

One natural approach for the substitution step is a lookup table: you feed in a fixed-width input, and the table gives you a scrambled output. These are called substitution boxes (S-boxes) — we will study them in depth when we reach AES, but think of them for now as pre-designed scramble tables. The most security-effective S-boxes tend to be non-invertible: they map a larger input (e.g. 6 bits) to a smaller output (e.g. 4 bits), deliberately discarding information to make the mapping harder to analyze. The problem: you cannot run such a table in reverse. How do you build a cipher that uses non-invertible operations but still decrypts correctly?

Horst Feistel's answer is one of the most elegant ideas in all of cryptography, and it is the foundation of DES.

The Analogy: One-Way Masking

Think of it this way. Alice writes her secret on the left page of a notebook. On the right page she writes some random-looking scribbles — a hash of the page number and a shared secret. She XORs the left page with those scribbles and sends both pages. Bob receives them. He can reproduce the exact same scribbles (same right page, same shared secret) and XOR again — because XOR is its own inverse. He recovers the left page without ever needing to "un-hash" anything. The Feistel structure does exactly this, with the two halves of a block, over many rounds.

The Core Mechanism: Split, Mix, Swap

Split the \(N\)-bit block into two equal halves. For each round \(i = 1, 2, \ldots, n\):

$$L_i = R_{i-1}$$ $$R_i = L_{i-1} \oplus F(R_{i-1},\; K_i)$$

In plain English: the right half passes through unchanged and becomes the new left half. The left half gets XORed with a scrambled version of the right half (via \(F\) and the round key \(K_i\)), and the result becomes the new right half.

The key freedom
\(F\) — the Feistel round function — can be anything: substitution tables, bit permutations, modular arithmetic. It is not required to be invertible. This freedom is the entire point of the design.

Deriving Decryption: A Step-by-Step Proof

Given the outputs of round \(i\) — that is, \((L_i,\; R_i)\) — and the round key \(K_i\), we want to recover the inputs \((L_{i-1},\; R_{i-1})\).

Step 1 — Recover \(R_{i-1}\):

The first encryption equation says \(L_i = R_{i-1}\). So trivially:

$$R_{i-1} = L_i$$

The old right half passed through unchanged — it is just sitting there as the current left half. No computation needed.

Step 2 — Recover \(L_{i-1}\):

The second encryption equation is:

$$R_i = L_{i-1} \oplus F(R_{i-1},\; K_i)$$

Substitute \(R_{i-1} = L_i\) from Step 1:

$$R_i = L_{i-1} \oplus F(L_i,\; K_i)$$

XOR both sides with \(F(L_i,\; K_i)\). Since \(x \oplus x = 0\) and \(x \oplus 0 = x\):

$$\boxed{L_{i-1} = R_i \oplus F(L_i,\; K_i)}$$

\(F\) is evaluated in the same forward direction as encryption. No inverse of \(F\) is ever needed.

The Punchline: Same Hardware, Reversed Keys

Decryption is structurally identical to encryption. The only difference: round keys are applied in reverse order.

DirectionRound key sequence
Encryption\(K_1,\; K_2,\; \ldots,\; K_{16}\)
Decryption\(K_{16},\; K_{15},\; \ldots,\; K_1\)

The same 16-round circuit that encrypts (with \(K_1 \to K_{16}\)) decrypts (with \(K_{16} \to K_1\)). No separate decrypt chip. No inverse S-box table. This was a major practical advantage when DES was standardised in 1977.

Coming up: Feistel vs. AES

When we study AES, you will see that it takes a fundamentally different structural approach — every transformation must have an explicit mathematical inverse, and the decryption algorithm is genuinely different from encryption. That contrast will make this section's elegance even clearer. For now, just hold onto the core insight: the Feistel structure gives you decryption for free, without requiring any step in \(F\) to be reversible.

❓ Why does the Feistel function F not need to be invertible?▾
Because \(F\) is never inverted during decryption. To recover \(L_{i-1}\) from \(R_i = L_{i-1} \oplus F(L_i, K_i)\), you simply XOR both sides with \(F(L_i, K_i)\) — evaluating \(F\) in the same forward direction. XOR's self-inverse property (\(x \oplus x = 0\)) does the cancellation. A substitution table that maps 6 bits to 4 bits is clearly non-invertible — yet a Feistel cipher using such a table decrypts perfectly.
❓ If F accidentally destroys some information (non-invertible), is the ciphertext still uniquely decryptable?▾
Yes. The decryptability of the Feistel cipher does not depend on \(F\) being invertible. Decryption recovers \(R_{i-1} = L_i\) trivially (the right half passed through), and then recovers \(L_{i-1} = R_i \oplus F(L_i, K_i)\) by re-applying \(F\) in the forward direction. The XOR cancels. Even if \(F\) loses information internally, the cipher's overall output is still uniquely invertible as long as the key is known.

Confusion and Diffusion — Shannon's Two Properties

In 1949, Claude Shannon — the founder of information theory — identified the two properties that make a cipher secure. These concepts are fundamental to understanding the design of both DES and AES.

Diffusion means that a change in any bit of the plaintext (or key) should affect as many bits as possible of the ciphertext. Ideally, flipping one plaintext bit should change, on average, half of all ciphertext bits. This destroys statistical structure: even if the plaintext has patterns (like English text), those patterns get "spread out" across the entire block. In DES, the permutation (transposition) operations achieve diffusion.

Confusion means that the relationship between the ciphertext and the key should be as complex as possible — each ciphertext bit should depend on many key bits in a complicated, non-linear way. This ensures that even observing many ciphertext samples doesn't reveal the key. In DES, the S-box substitution operations achieve confusion.

You need both properties together. Consider a cipher with only diffusion (pure transposition): the attacker still sees the same set of bits — just rearranged — and single-letter frequencies are completely preserved. Now consider only confusion (pure substitution): the cipher applies substitution independently to small groups, so patterns can re-emerge. The combination — substitution creating confusion, then permutation spreading that confusion across the whole block — produces the avalanche effect: a single input bit change cascades through multiple rounds, affecting approximately half of all output bits.

In DES, a single plaintext bit change affects an average of 31 bit positions in the ciphertext after 16 rounds. AES, which we study next, achieves a much stronger avalanche — all 128 output bit positions change — thanks to a more effective combination of confusion and diffusion steps. We will understand exactly why once we cover AES's design in detail.

Why DES Was Retired

DES's 56-bit key was its critical weakness. When DES was designed in the 1970s, 2⁵⁶ keys seemed safely beyond reach. By the late 1990s, hardware had improved dramatically. In 1998, the Electronic Frontier Foundation built "Deep Crack" for $250,000 — a dedicated machine that cracked DES in under 56 hours. Today, the same attack would cost a few hundred dollars using cloud computing.

AES — The Big Picture

Before diving into any individual operation, build the mental model first. AES uses a Substitution-Permutation Network (SPN): every round transforms the entire 128-bit block through the same four operations in sequence. Unlike a Feistel cipher (which leaves half the block untouched per round), an SPN transforms every byte every round — stronger security per round, in fewer rounds.

Each of the four operations exists to solve exactly one specific cryptographic problem:

Problem 1: Without non-linear operations, the cipher is algebraically breakable.

If all operations are linear (shifts, XOR, matrix multiply), an attacker sets up a system of linear equations and solves for the key — exactly how the Hill cipher breaks. SubBytes fixes this: it is the only non-linear step, using a lookup table built from finite field mathematics to eliminate any simple algebraic relationship between input and output.

Problem 2: After SubBytes, bytes in different columns still never interact.

SubBytes substitutes each byte independently. Without inter-column mixing, an attacker attacks each column separately (4 independent 32-bit problems instead of one 128-bit problem). ShiftRows fixes this by offset-shifting rows, so that each column now contains bytes from four different original columns — setting up MixColumns to achieve global mixing.

Problem 3: After ShiftRows, bytes have been rearranged but not actually mixed together.

Rearranging bytes doesn't make them depend on each other — a column still holds four independent values. MixColumns fixes this: matrix multiplication over GF(2⁸) ensures every output byte in a column depends on all four input bytes (MDS property).

Problem 4: All three operations above are public — anyone can run them.

Without the secret key, AES would produce the same output for every user. AddRoundKey fixes this: it XORs the state with a key-derived value at every round, tying the computation irrevocably to the secret.

The round order — SubBytes → ShiftRows → MixColumns → AddRoundKey — is deliberate: SubBytes creates confusion per byte, ShiftRows + MixColumns together spread that confusion across the full block, and AddRoundKey ties everything to the secret key.

The Round Structure

Pre-whitening: before any round, XOR state with \(w_0\text{–}w_3\) (initial key addition — ensures plaintext is never exposed unencrypted).

Rounds 1–9: SubBytes → ShiftRows → MixColumns → AddRoundKey

Round 10 (final): SubBytes → ShiftRows → AddRoundKey (no MixColumns — it would be immediately undone in decryption with zero security gain)

❓ Why does AES apply MixColumns before AddRoundKey, not after?▾
If AddRoundKey came before MixColumns, the XOR with the key would be "undone" by MixColumns — specifically, because MixColumns is linear: MixColumns(state ⊕ key) = MixColumns(state) ⊕ MixColumns(key). An attacker could absorb the key XOR into the MixColumns step and analyze the cipher without knowing the key. Applying AddRoundKey last, after MixColumns, ensures the key mixing cannot be separated from the diffusion step algebraically.

The State Array

AES organizes its 128-bit (16-byte) input into a 4×4 state array, filled column-major: bytes go down column 0 first, then column 1, etc.

Input bytes:  b₀  b₁  b₂  b₃  b₄  b₅  b₆  b₇  b₈  b₉  b₁₀ b₁₁ b₁₂ b₁₃ b₁₄ b₁₅

State array (column-major — fill DOWN then RIGHT):
         Col 0    Col 1    Col 2    Col 3
Row 0:    b₀       b₄       b₈      b₁₂
Row 1:    b₁       b₅       b₉      b₁₃
Row 2:    b₂       b₆      b₁₀      b₁₄
Row 3:    b₃       b₇      b₁₁      b₁₅

Why a 4×4 matrix? Because each of the four round operations is designed around this shape: ShiftRows operates on rows, MixColumns operates on columns, and SubBytes + AddRoundKey operate on individual bytes. The 4×4 grid makes all four operations natural and efficient. Output is read back column-major after all rounds.

GF(2⁸) Arithmetic — The Shared Mathematical Foundation

Both SubBytes (S-box construction) and MixColumns rely on arithmetic in GF(2⁸) — the Galois Field with 256 elements, one per possible byte value. It sounds daunting, but you only need three practical rules for this course.

What GF(2⁸) Is

GF(2⁸) is a mathematical field where addition and multiplication are defined for all 256 byte values (0x00–0xFF), and every non-zero byte has a multiplicative inverse. The "special sauce" is that all arithmetic is performed with polynomials whose coefficients are 0 or 1 (mod 2) — which maps perfectly onto bits. When multiplication would exceed 8 bits, the result is reduced modulo the irreducible polynomial \(x^8 + x^4 + x^3 + x + 1\) (hex 0x11b). Think of this exactly like clock arithmetic mod 12 — you stay within the 8-bit range.

Rule 1: Addition = XOR

Adding two bytes in GF(2⁸) is ordinary bitwise XOR. No carry, no overflow.

$$a \oplus b$$

Example: \(0\text{x}53 \oplus 0\text{xCA} = 0\text{x}99\)

Rule 2: Multiplication by 2 (xtime)

Multiplying a byte \(b\) by 2 in GF(2⁸):

$$\text{xtime}(b) = \begin{cases} b \ll 1 & \text{if bit 7 of } b = 0 \\ (b \ll 1) \oplus 0\text{x}1\text{b} & \text{if bit 7 of } b = 1 \end{cases}$$

Left-shift by 1. If the original bit 7 was 1 (the shift would overflow 8 bits), XOR with 0x1b — this is the reduction step, subtracting the irreducible polynomial's lower 8 bits \((x^4+x^3+x+1 = 0\text{x}1\text{b})\) to stay within one byte.

Example 1: \(\text{xtime}(0\text{x}57)\): \(0\text{x}57 = 0101\,0111_2\). Bit 7 = 0. Shift: \(0\text{xAE}\). No XOR. Result: \(\mathbf{0\text{xAE}}\).
Example 2: \(\text{xtime}(0\text{xAE})\): \(0\text{xAE} = 1010\,1110_2\). Bit 7 = 1. Shift: \(0\text{x5C}\). XOR \(0\text{x1b}\): \(0\text{x5C} \oplus 0\text{x1b} = 0\text{x47}\). Result: \(\mathbf{0\text{x47}}\).

Rule 3: Multiplication by 3

$$3 \times b \;=\; \text{xtime}(b) \;\oplus\; b$$

Apply xtime once, then XOR with the original byte. Example: \(3 \times 0\text{x}57 = 0\text{xAE} \oplus 0\text{x}57 = 0\text{xF9}\).

That's all you need
Addition = XOR. Multiplication by 1 = identity. Multiplication by 2 = xtime. Multiplication by 3 = xtime ⊕ original. These three rules let you evaluate any MixColumns computation by hand. Higher multiplications (×9, ×11, ×13, ×14 for InvMixColumns) are just repeated xtime applications chained together.
❓ Compute xtime(0xD4). Show your work.▾
\(0\text{xD4} = 1101\,0100_2\). Bit 7 = 1. Left-shift: \(1010\,1000_2 = 0\text{xA8}\). Since bit 7 was 1, XOR with \(0\text{x1b}\): \(0\text{xA8} \oplus 0\text{x1b} = 0\text{xB3}\). Result: 0xB3.

The Key Schedule — Why Every Decision Was Made This Way

Why Not Use the Master Key Every Round?

If every round used the same master key, the cipher would have dangerous symmetry: patterns in round 1 would repeat identically in rounds 5 and 9. An attacker could XOR the inputs and outputs of corresponding rounds to cancel the key, effectively attacking each round in isolation. Different round keys eliminate this symmetry — changing any single master key bit must cascade unpredictably to many round keys.

How Many Round Keys?

AES-128 needs: 1 pre-whitening key + 1 per each of 10 rounds = 11 four-word round keys = 44 words total. Since the master key is 4 words, the key schedule must expand 4 words into 44.

The Two-Case Expansion

\(i\) is NOT a multiple of 4 — simple XOR chain:

$$w[i] = w[i-4] \oplus w[i-1]$$

\(i\) IS a multiple of 4 — apply special function \(g\):

$$w[i] = w[i-4] \oplus g(w[i-1])$$
$$w_4 = w_0 \oplus g(w_3), \quad w_5 = w_1 \oplus w_4, \quad w_6 = w_2 \oplus w_5, \quad w_7 = w_3 \oplus w_6$$

Why g() at Multiples of 4 — and Not Every Word?

Without g(), \(w[i] = w[i-4] \oplus w[i-1]\) is a linear recurrence. A purely linear key schedule means the entire expanded key is an affine function of the master key — vulnerable to related-key attacks where an attacker encrypts under two related keys and exploits their algebraic relationship. To break this linearity, AES injects g() once per group of 4 words — one non-linear anchor per group is sufficient. Applying g() to every word would be redundant and slow; applying it only at multiples of 4 is the minimum needed.

Why Each Step of g()?

① RotWord: \([a_0,a_1,a_2,a_3] \to [a_1,a_2,a_3,a_0]\)

$$[a_0,\; a_1,\; a_2,\; a_3] \;\longrightarrow\; [a_1,\; a_2,\; a_3,\; a_0]$$

Without rotation, byte position 0 of the last word in group k would always feed byte position 0 of the first word in group k+1 — a fixed alignment enabling byte-by-byte attacks on the key schedule. Rotation shuffles byte positions, breaking this alignment.

② SubWord: apply S-box to each byte

RotWord is still a linear operation. Without SubWord, the key schedule remains algebraically exploitable even with rotation. SubWord applies the same S-box used in SubBytes — non-linear by construction — making the key schedule resistant to the same algebraic attacks that SubBytes resists in the cipher itself. One mathematical structure, two security benefits.

③ XOR with Rcon:

$$\oplus\; [\text{Rcon}[j],\; 0\text{x}00,\; 0\text{x}00,\; 0\text{x}00]$$

\(\text{Rcon}[1]=0\text{x}01,\; \text{Rcon}[2]=0\text{x}02,\; \text{Rcon}[3]=0\text{x}04,\; \ldots\) (doubles each time in GF(2⁸)). Even with RotWord and SubWord, two g() calls receiving the same input could theoretically produce the same output — causing round keys to repeat. Rcon injects a unique constant into each g() call, guaranteeing all 10 applications produce distinct outputs regardless of the master key value.

SubBytes — Confusion Through Non-Linear Substitution

Why SubBytes Is the Only Non-Linear Step

Every other AES operation is linear: ShiftRows permutes bytes, MixColumns is matrix multiplication, AddRoundKey is XOR. A cipher built entirely from linear operations is algebraically breakable: given enough plaintext–ciphertext pairs, an attacker sets up and solves a linear system to recover the key — exactly what destroys the Hill cipher. SubBytes is the sole non-linear operation in AES, placed first in each round so its non-linearity propagates through ShiftRows, MixColumns, and AddRoundKey.

The S-box Lookup

For each of the 16 bytes in the state: the byte's upper 4 bits (high nibble) give the row index; lower 4 bits (low nibble) give the column index. Look up the 16×16 S-box at (row, column) and replace the byte. In hardware: 16 parallel table lookups — very fast.

Example: byte \(0\text{x}95\) → row 9, column 5 → S-box[9][5].

Why These Specific S-box Values? — Two-Step Construction (Assignment 2, Q1)

The S-box is not random. It is built by a precise two-step process for maximum non-linearity and provable security.

Step 1 — Multiplicative inverse in GF(2⁸): For each non-zero byte \(b\), find \(b^{-1}\) such that \(b \times b^{-1} = 1\) in GF(2⁸) (using \(x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1\)). The byte \(0\text{x}00\) maps to \(0\text{x}00\) by convention (no inverse exists). This mapping is maximally non-linear — no polynomial of degree less than 8 over GF(2) approximates it — which is exactly the property needed for the non-linear substitution step. (GF(2⁸) arithmetic rules were established in the previous section.)

Step 2 — Affine bit transformation: For each bit \(b_i\) (\(i=0\ldots 7\)) of the inverse byte:

$$b'_i \;=\; b_i \;\oplus\; b_{(i+4)\bmod 8} \;\oplus\; b_{(i+5)\bmod 8} \;\oplus\; b_{(i+6)\bmod 8} \;\oplus\; b_{(i+7)\bmod 8} \;\oplus\; c_i$$

where \(c = 0\text{x}63 = 01100011_2\). Why \(0\text{x}63\)? Without this constant, \(0\text{x}00\) (whose inverse is \(0\text{x}00\)) passes through unchanged — a fixed point. The constant ensures \(0\text{x}00 \mapsto 0\text{x}63\), and the full combined mapping has no fixed points or anti-fixed points.

S-box Construction Summary
  1. Initialize 16×16 table: cell \((r,c)\) contains byte \(\text{0xrc}\)
  2. Replace each non-zero byte with its GF(2⁸) multiplicative inverse using \(x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1\). (\(0\text{x}00 \to 0\text{x}00\))
  3. Apply affine transform with constant \(0\text{x}63\). Eliminates fixed points, adds constant non-linearity.

InvSubBytes: reverse order — (1) inverse affine with constant \(0\text{x}05\), then (2) GF(2⁸) multiplicative inverse.

ShiftRows — Breaking Column Independence

The Problem ShiftRows Solves

Imagine AES without ShiftRows: SubBytes substitutes each byte independently, then MixColumns mixes within each column. But the four columns would remain completely independent throughout all 10 rounds — an attacker could attack each 32-bit column separately, reducing a 128-bit problem to four independent 32-bit problems. ShiftRows breaks this by offset-shifting rows before MixColumns runs, so that each column now contains bytes from four different original columns.

How It Works

Cyclic left shifts, with a different amount per row:

Before ShiftRows:         After ShiftRows:
s₀₀ s₀₁ s₀₂ s₀₃         s₀₀ s₀₁ s₀₂ s₀₃   ← row 0: no shift
s₁₀ s₁₁ s₁₂ s₁₃   →     s₁₁ s₁₂ s₁₃ s₁₀   ← row 1: left 1
s₂₀ s₂₁ s₂₂ s₂₃         s₂₂ s₂₃ s₂₀ s₂₁   ← row 2: left 2
s₃₀ s₃₁ s₃₂ s₃₃         s₃₃ s₃₀ s₃₁ s₃₂   ← row 3: left 3

New column 0 = \(\{s_{00}, s_{11}, s_{22}, s_{33}\}\) — one byte from each original column. When MixColumns processes column 0, it mixes bytes from all four original columns. InvShiftRows (decryption) reverses this with right shifts of the same amounts.

The Two-Round Avalanche

ShiftRows alone only rearranges bytes — no mixing. MixColumns alone mixes within one column. Together, across two rounds: Round 1 → one changed byte spreads to 4 bytes in its column (via MixColumns) → ShiftRows distributes those 4 bytes into 4 different columns. Round 2 → MixColumns processes each of those 4 columns → \(4 \times 4 = 16\) bytes affected. After 10 rounds: all 128 output bits depend on the changed input bit.

❓ Why does ShiftRows use different shift amounts (0, 1, 2, 3) instead of all the same?▾
If all rows shifted by the same amount, each column would still contain only bytes from one original column — just from different rows. The different amounts (0, 1, 2, 3) ensure each new column contains exactly one byte from each of the four original columns, which is what enables MixColumns to achieve full inter-column mixing.

MixColumns — Column-Level Diffusion

What MixColumns Does

MixColumns processes each of the four columns independently. It treats the 4 bytes of a column as a vector and multiplies it by a fixed \(4\times 4\) matrix — all arithmetic in GF(2⁸) (addition = XOR; ×2 = xtime; ×3 = xtime ⊕ original, as covered in the GF(2⁸) section). The result: every output byte depends on all four input bytes. Change one input byte → all four output bytes change. This is the MDS (Maximum Distance Separable) property of the chosen matrix.

The Matrix

$$\begin{bmatrix} s'_0 \\ s'_1 \\ s'_2 \\ s'_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 3 & 1 & 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} s_0 \\ s_1 \\ s_2 \\ s_3 \end{bmatrix}$$
$$s'_0 = (2 \times s_0) \oplus (3 \times s_1) \oplus s_2 \oplus s_3$$ $$s'_1 = s_0 \oplus (2 \times s_1) \oplus (3 \times s_2) \oplus s_3$$ $$s'_2 = s_0 \oplus s_1 \oplus (2 \times s_2) \oplus (3 \times s_3)$$ $$s'_3 = (3 \times s_0) \oplus s_1 \oplus s_2 \oplus (2 \times s_3)$$
Why coefficients 1, 2, 3?

(1) Cheapest to compute: ×1 is free, ×2 is one xtime, ×3 is xtime ⊕ original — the minimum-cost GF(2⁸) multiplications. (2) MDS property: this specific \(\{1,2,3\}\) pattern guarantees that changing any single input byte changes all four output bytes — the strongest possible column diffusion for a \(4\times4\) matrix using these coefficients.

InvMixColumns (decryption) uses the inverse matrix with coefficients \(\{14, 11, 13, 9\}\):

$$\begin{bmatrix} s_0 \\ s_1 \\ s_2 \\ s_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 14 & 11 & 13 & 9 \\ 9 & 14 & 11 & 13 \\ 13 & 9 & 14 & 11 \\ 11 & 13 & 9 & 14 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} s'_0 \\ s'_1 \\ s'_2 \\ s'_3 \end{bmatrix}$$

AddRoundKey — Introducing the Secret

Why It Exists

SubBytes, ShiftRows, and MixColumns are all public, deterministic operations. Without AddRoundKey, AES would produce the same output for every user with the same input — anyone who knows the algorithm could decrypt any message. AddRoundKey is where the secret key enters every round, making the computation unique to the key holder.

What It Does

XOR the entire 4×4 state byte-by-byte with the current round's 4-word (16-byte) round key:

$$\text{state}[r][c] \;=\; \text{state}[r][c] \;\oplus\; \text{roundkey}[r][c]$$

It is the conceptually simplest of the four operations — just XOR — but the most security-critical: it is the only step tied to the secret.

Why XOR?

XOR is self-inverse: \(A \oplus K \oplus K = A\). The AddRoundKey step in decryption is identical to encryption — XOR the same round key again and it undoes itself. No separate inverse operation is needed. This is why AddRoundKey is the one AES operation that is exactly the same in both the encryption and decryption algorithms.

AddRoundKey and Confusion

After AddRoundKey, every state byte depends on the round key byte. Each round key byte was derived non-linearly from the master key through the key schedule (RotWord + SubWord + XOR). So each state byte indirectly depends on many master key bits in a complex way — this is Shannon's confusion property.

⚠️ Critical Exam Distinction (Q5)

The key schedule only generates round keys. AddRoundKey applies them and creates confusion. These are two separate operations. Q5 answer = (a): "expand the master key into multiple round keys."

Full AES Encryption & Decryption Flow

Complete Encryption Flow

Input: 128-bit plaintext
  ↓
1. Arrange as 4×4 state (column-major)
  ↓
2. AddRoundKey with w[0..3]           ← pre-whitening
  ↓
3. For rounds 1 to 9:
   a. SubBytes       (non-linear substitution — confusion)
   b. ShiftRows      (row offsets for cross-column mixing)
   c. MixColumns     (GF(2⁸) matrix multiply per column — diffusion)
   d. AddRoundKey    (XOR with w[4i..4i+3] — key injection)
  ↓
4. Round 10 (no MixColumns):
   a. SubBytes
   b. ShiftRows
   c. AddRoundKey with w[40..43]
  ↓
Output: 128-bit ciphertext (read column-major)
Why no MixColumns in round 10?
Including it would require InvMixColumns at the start of the corresponding decryption round — adding computation with zero security benefit (the two operations cancel). Omitting it saves a step in both encryption and decryption.

Complete Decryption Flow

AES decryption is not encryption reversed — it requires explicit inverse operations because every SPN operation has a mathematical inverse (unlike the Feistel structure which is self-inverse by design):

Input: 128-bit ciphertext
  ↓
1. Arrange as 4×4 state
  ↓
2. AddRoundKey with w[40..43]         ← undo last encryption step first
  ↓
3. For rounds 9 down to 1:
   a. InvShiftRows   (right shifts: 0, 1, 2, 3 bytes per row)
   b. InvSubBytes    (inverse S-box lookup)
   c. AddRoundKey    (XOR same round key — identical to encryption, XOR is self-inverse)
   d. InvMixColumns  (inverse matrix multiply)
  ↓
4. Final round (no InvMixColumns):
   a. InvShiftRows
   b. InvSubBytes
   c. AddRoundKey with w[0..3]
  ↓
Output: 128-bit plaintext
❓ How does AES decryption structurally differ from DES decryption?▾
DES (Feistel): same algorithm for both directions — only key order reverses. The Feistel XOR structure undoes itself. No explicit inverse operations needed.

AES (SPN): every operation has an explicit mathematical inverse — InvSubBytes, InvShiftRows, InvMixColumns — applied in a different sequence. AES decryption is a genuinely different algorithm from encryption. This is the price of the SPN's stronger per-round mixing.

❓ Large key space: necessary but not sufficient — why?▾
Key space determines only brute-force resistance. The monoalphabetic cipher has 26!≈4×10²⁶ keys (larger than DES) but is broken in minutes by frequency analysis. Security requires large key space AND destruction of statistical/algebraic structure.

It would be immediately undone by InvMixColumns at the start of the corresponding decryption round — adding computation without security. The final round's security comes entirely from AddRoundKey.
